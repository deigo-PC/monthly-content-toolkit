# Claims Policy

This file is short on purpose. Read it fully, every time, before writing facts into a blog post.

---

## The rule

**Never invent. Always check the knowledgebase first. If it's genuinely not there, omit or
generalize — don't stop the batch, don't ask, just keep moving.**

This applies to:
- Prices
- Guarantees
- Certifications
- Years in business
- Specific locations
- Legal claims
- Medical claims
- Financial claims
- Client names
- Case studies
- Performance results / statistics
- Awards
- Exact timelines

---

## Before assuming something isn't available, actually look

The brand's Project knowledgebase often *does* contain real versions of these details — they just
live in documents that aren't the obvious "facts" file. Check before concluding something's
missing:

- **Marketing Brain outputs** (Genesis Report, Master Strategy Doc, buyer psychology reports) often
  contain founding story details, real differentiators, and documented trust signals
- **Message Block Library** often has brand-approved phrasing for claims that ARE allowed to be made
- **Raw Intelligence Doc** may contain market data, competitor context, or sourced statistics
- **Any uploaded service pages, pricing pages, or FAQ docs** in the knowledgebase
- **Past content** in the knowledgebase may show how the brand has phrased a claim before

If a real, sourced detail exists anywhere in the knowledgebase — use it, and use it accurately. The
rule isn't "never mention prices." It's "never invent a price that isn't documented anywhere."

---

## When something genuinely isn't there

If you've actually checked and a detail the post would benefit from (e.g. a specific price, a
named certification, a performance statistic) isn't documented anywhere in the knowledgebase:

1. **Omit it**, or **phrase generally** instead (e.g. "flexible financing options are available"
   instead of inventing a specific rate or term)
2. **Note it** in the post's Notes / Assumptions section — name exactly what's missing so it's easy
   to confirm before publishing (e.g. "Pricing for this service isn't in the knowledgebase — the
   post avoids specific numbers; confirm before publishing if you want a price range included.")
3. **Keep moving** — don't pause the batch or ask before continuing to the next blog post. Flag and
   proceed.

---

## What this is not

This isn't a rule against ever stating facts. A brand's real, documented years-in-business, a real
certification it holds, or a real statistic from its own Marketing Brain research should absolutely
appear in the post when relevant — sourced and accurate. The failure mode this policy prevents is
writing something that *sounds* specific and confident but was never actually confirmed anywhere.
